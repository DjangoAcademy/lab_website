Dr. Ben Hamlington

Ben Hamlington is an assistant professor in the Ocean, Earth, and Atmospheric Sciences Department at Old Dominion University. Prior to arriving at ODU, Ben obtained his BS and MS in Mechanical Engineering from Washington University in St. Louis, and his PhD in Aerospace Engineering Sciences from the University of Colorado at Boulder. His primary research interest is on past, present and future sea level rise, focusing on the use of satellite observations to improve our understanding of the ocean. Beyond studying sea level, Ben does research on a wide range of topics including ENSO/climate variability, tsunamis, wildfires, and land subsidence.

Lauren Sommers, M.S.

My research interests include: climate change, sea level rise, circulation, modeling, bio-physical interactions, and applied mathematics. I enjoy work that is interdisciplinary where I can work with a variety of people on projects that include both observational work and modeling. I am currently working on developing estimating factors effecting sea level change and an index on regional sea level rise. I completed two bachelor of science degrees from California State University Monterey Bay in mathematics (2015) and environmental science, technology and, policy (2015), and received a master of science degree in oceanography from Old Dominion University (2017). I am enjoying living on a new coast and learning about coastal processes and different ecosystems that exist here in Virginia. Alessandra Burgos

I am broadly interested in all aspects of climate change, from polar amplification to sea level rise. The atmosphere and ocean are closely interconnected and determining those interconnections are vital to understand the inner-workings of how anthropogenic forcings will continue to affect our world. Currently I am working on a 20th century regional sea level rise trend map, to help quantify sea level rates from the past century. This will help put into context future sea level rise and will improve future estimates as well. Moving forward, I plan to continue research on how sea level rise may have influenced extreme coastal events along the Mid-Atlantic shore.
